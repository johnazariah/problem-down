# Reviewer Notes
**Date:** April 16, 2026
**Agent:** Claude Opus 4.6 (1M context)
**Manuscript:** *The Problem With Quantum* — first draft, 117 pages, 19 chapters

---

## Overall Assessment

The manuscript delivers on its central promise: start from problems, pull in only the quantum machinery needed to solve them. The three-act structure (encode → interfere → measure) is established in Unit 1 and traced through all eight units, culminating in a conclusion that names interference as the single unifying idea. This is strong.

The dual-path design (Units for executives, Deep-Dives for practitioners) works well through the first three units. It degrades in the later units, where the application chapters grow thinner and the deep-dives either rush or assume accumulated comfort that not every reader will have.

The writing voice is at its best when it is direct and concrete: truth tables, box-level circuits, the "arrows" metaphor for interference. It is at its worst when it lapses into name-dropping (algorithm taxonomies, citation-heavy Reality Checks) or rushes through formalism without motivation.

---

## Severity A — Will Lose Readers

### A1. Unit 5 (Finance): Grover + QAE compressed too tightly

The Quantum Angle section introduces Grover's algorithm, amplitude amplification, the Grover iterator, the connection between the angle θ and the fraction of solutions, and quantum amplitude estimation — all in ~500 words. This is the densest passage in the book. The CEO reader needs Grover explained at the same pace as QAOA was in Unit 1. The current text reads like a lecture outline, not a narrative.

*Recommendation:* Expand the Grover intuition ("rotation, not search") from Deep-Dive 5 into Unit 5's Quantum Angle. Give the reader the geometric picture *before* jumping to QAE.

### A2. Unit 1 (Logistics): The Hook lists four classical heuristics cold

"Branch-and-bound, genetic algorithms, simulated annealing, Lin-Kernighan" — a wall of names. Only simulated annealing earns its keep later (it's explained in Unit 6 and contrasted with quantum annealing). The other three are never mentioned again.

*Recommendation:* Cut to "a toolkit of sophisticated heuristics, from simulated annealing to specialised graph algorithms." Name one, gesture at the rest.

### A3. Unit 1 (Logistics): Reality Check reads like a conference paper

The Reality Check section name-drops Basso et al., Farhi et al. (2025), Jordan et al. (Nature, 2025), Azariah and Jordan (2026), DQI+BP, tree tensor networks, and Walsh-Hadamard factorisation. A CEO needs: (1) QAOA underperforms at low depth, (2) it's competitive at high depth, (3) hardware can't run high depth yet, (4) this is a hardware problem, not an algorithm problem. Everything else belongs in Chef's Notes or Further Reading.

*Recommendation:* Strip the Reality Check to four paragraphs (low depth, high depth, hardware gap, barren plateaus). Move the citation-heavy analysis to Chef's Notes.

### A4. Bloch sphere used before defined

Deep-Dive 1 uses "Bloch sphere" (in the R_X description), and Deep-Dive 4 (quantum kernels) uses it as if the reader already has the picture. It's never actually explained — the reader doesn't know it's a sphere on which single-qubit states live.

*Recommendation:* Add a one-sentence definition at first use in Deep-Dive 1: "A single qubit's state can be visualised as a point on a sphere — the **Bloch sphere** — where $|0\rangle$ is the north pole, $|1\rangle$ is the south pole, and superpositions live on the surface between."

### A5. Deep-Dive 5 (Amplitude Estimation): outer product introduced in a formula

The diffusion operator $S_0 = 2|s\rangle\langle s| - I$ introduces the outer product notation. While there's now an inline explanation, it's embedded in the middle of a formula-heavy paragraph. The CEO wouldn't be here, but the grad student is encountering a new algebraic object (operators from outer products) for the first time in a passage that's also introducing the Grover iterator geometry. Two new ideas in one paragraph.

*Recommendation:* Separate the outer-product explanation into its own callout box, analogous to the Superposition and Interference boxes in Unit 1.

## Severity B — Friction, But Recoverable

### B1. Unit 2 (Cryptography): Reality Check citations in running text

"Gidney and Ekerå (2021)," "Webster, Berent, Chandra, and Hockings (arXiv:2602.11457)," "Kim, Jang, et al. (ePrint 2026/106)" — three inline citations in three consecutive paragraphs. The Reality Check for Unit 2 is more careful than Unit 1's (terms are defined), but the citation density still reads like an academic paper rather than a book chapter.

*Recommendation:* Move author names to parenthetical or footnotes. The reader needs the *result* (100K qubits, not 20M), not the *attribution* inline.

### B2. Unit 3 (Drug Discovery): classical chemistry hierarchy table

The table (HF / DFT / MP2 / CCSD(T) / Full CI) is well-constructed and the "What it does" column added during editing helps. But five acronyms in five rows is still heavy. The CEO reader likely only needs to know: simple methods miss correlation, the gold standard scales as $N^7$, and the exact method is exponential.

*Recommendation:* Consider reducing to three rows (HF, CCSD(T), Full CI) with the others mentioned in a prose sentence. Or add a "you are here" annotation making clear which row is the target.

### B3. Unit 4 (Machine Learning): honest but underwhelming

The Reality Check correctly identifies ML as the most speculative application area. The problem is that the Unit doesn't earn enough engagement before arriving at "we honestly don't know." The worked example is a pointer to a notebook with no in-chapter data (unlike Units 1–3 which have probability tables or energy curves). The CEO finishes Unit 4 thinking "so quantum ML is a maybe?" — which is accurate but unsatisfying.

*Recommendation:* Add a concrete result from the notebook to the worked example (e.g., decision boundary comparison, classification accuracy). Give the reader something to *see*.

### B4. Deep-Dive 2 (Inside Shor's): Bernstein-Vazirani and Simon name-dropped without payoff

The text correctly labels these as "stepping stones toward Shor's" and says "we won't detail them here." Good — but then why name them? A grad student who doesn't already know these algorithms gains nothing from the names. A grad student who does know them doesn't need the reminder.

*Recommendation:* Either cut to "similar algorithms with more structured problems use the same phase-kickback pattern" or give each a one-sentence operational description (e.g., "Bernstein-Vazirani recovers a hidden binary string in one query").

### B5. Deep-Dive 6 (QUBO): the equality constraint expansion

The algebraic expansion of $(\sum_n x_{n,s} - 1)^2$ is shown in full but not narrated. The reader sees the formula go from one line to two with a "using $x_i^2 = x_i$ for binary variables" aside. The binary-variable property is the key insight (squaring a 0/1 variable is a no-op) but it's buried in a parenthetical.

*Recommendation:* Give the $x^2 = x$ trick its own sentence before the expansion: "For binary variables, there's a useful simplification: $x^2 = x$ (because $0^2 = 0$ and $1^2 = 1$). This means any polynomial in binary variables can be reduced to degree 2 or less."

### B6. Deep-Dive 7 (QPE & Trotter): qubitization section reads like a literature review

The qubitization section (added late) introduces block encoding, QSP, and QSVT in rapid succession with no worked example or intuition. For a grad student, this is useful as a pointer, but it doesn't match the pedagogical depth of the rest of the deep-dive. It reads like an afterthought rather than an integrated section.

*Recommendation:* Either expand with a concrete example (what does "block encoding" actually mean — encoding H as a sub-block of a larger unitary) or trim to a single paragraph pointing to the references. The current middle ground serves neither audience well.

### B7. Unit 7 (Materials Science): Hubbard Hamiltonian notation jump

Despite the improvements (glossing $c_{i\sigma}^\dagger$, h.c., number operator, $\langle i,j \rangle$), the Hamiltonian equation is still the single biggest notation dump in the book. Six new notational elements in one equation. The CEO reader encounters this after comfortable prose about superconductors and room-temperature dreams.

*Recommendation:* Build to the full equation in stages. First show the hopping term alone with words: "electrons hop between neighbouring sites; the amplitude of hopping is $t$." Then show the interaction term: "two electrons on the same site pay an energy cost $U$." Then combine. The full equation is the punchline, not the starting point.

### B8. Unit 8 (Climate): d-orbitals, π/π*, open-shell, multi-reference

The Bottleneck section introduces four chemistry terms in two paragraphs: d-orbitals (now glossed), π/π* orbitals (now glossed), open-shell systems, and multi-reference states. The glosses help, but the density is still high for a CEO reader who last saw chemistry in college.

*Recommendation:* Consolidate to one key idea: "The electrons in the catalyst's active site are strongly correlated — the same problem from Unit 3, but in a messier environment." The specific orbital labels can live in the Deep-Dive.

### B9. Cross-cutting: "barren plateaus" inconsistently explained

Barren plateaus are mentioned in Unit 1 (Reality Check, no definition), Unit 3 (defined), Unit 4 (mentioned), Deep-Dive 3 (defined again). The reader who takes the fast path (Units only) hits the term in Unit 1 without a definition, gets a definition in Unit 3, and then it's assumed knowledge.

*Recommendation:* Define at first use (Unit 1) or defer to Unit 3 where it's more relevant.

### B10. Cross-cutting: "tapering" used four times, barely explained

Tapering (exploiting symmetries to reduce qubit count) appears in Units 3, 7, 8, and Deep-Dive 8. The Unit 3 mention adds a parenthetical, but the reader who encounters it in Unit 7 or 8 may not remember.

*Recommendation:* Consistent parenthetical at every use: "tapering (exploiting symmetries to reduce qubit count)."

## Severity C — Polish

### C1. Unit 1: "cost landscape" used before defined
The Bottleneck section uses "rugged cost landscape" as if the reader already has this mental model. It's actually quite evocative — but a one-sentence grounding ("imagine a mountainous terrain where altitude represents cost, and you're trying to find the lowest valley") would land it.

### C2. Unit 2: "harvest now, decrypt later" is great
This phrase in the Reality Check is one of the best in the book. It makes the threat visceral for a CEO. Keep it exactly as is.

### C3. Deep-Dive 1: "Common Mistake #1" and "#2" boxes
These work well as a pedagogical device. Consider extending the pattern to other Deep-Dives where appropriate — DD2 has them, but DD3 onward only has one or none.

### C4. The teleportation circuit in Unit 1
Introduced as a circuit-reading exercise, never returned to. The text says "we'll return to teleportation in later units" — but we don't. Either cut the forward reference or make good on it.

### C5. Conclusion: "interference machine" framing
The conclusion's thesis — "every quantum algorithm is an interference machine" — is the best single sentence in the book. It should arguably appear earlier (perhaps as a callout in Unit 1's interference box) so the reader carries it throughout.

### C6. QEC chapter: the marble-in-a-bowl analogy is missing
The adiabatic theorem (Unit 6) and the QEC chapter both describe systems staying in ground states or being nudged out. A simple physical analogy — a marble in a bowl that stays at the bottom as you slowly tilt it — would help both sections.

### C7. Preface: "Let's start with a delivery truck."
Perfect closing line. Keep it.

### C8. $\sigma$ overloading in Unit 5
Standard deviation $\sigma$ in the Bottleneck and volatility $\sigma$ in the Worked Example are related (volatility *is* a standard deviation of returns) but the reuse of the symbol without acknowledgment may confuse.

### C9. The word "circuit" means two things
In the classical sections, "circuit" could refer to electronic circuits. In the quantum sections, it means a sequence of gates. The context usually disambiguates, but a parenthetical "(a quantum circuit is a sequence of gate operations, not an electronic circuit)" on first use would prevent any confusion.

### C10. Deep-Dive 8 is the strongest capstone
The "boring orbitals" language and the "every box references a chapter" pipeline diagram tie the whole book together. This chapter doesn't need significant changes.

## Structural Notes

### Word count imbalance
| Group | Avg words |
|-------|-----------|
| Units 1–3 | ~3,400 |
| Deep-Dives 1–3 | ~2,900 |
| Units 4–8 | ~1,700 |
| Deep-Dives 4–8 | ~1,200 |

Units 4–8 are roughly half the length of Units 1–3. This is partly natural (later units can lean on established vocabulary) but the effect is that the book's second half feels rushed. Units 5 (Finance) and 7 (Materials) would benefit most from expansion.

### Missing entanglement moment
Entanglement is introduced as a one-liner with CNOT ("correlated in ways that have no classical analogue") and used mechanically throughout. It never gets its own conceptual moment — the reader never learns *why* entanglement is a resource, only that it exists. The teleportation circuit was an opportunity for this but is treated as a circuit-reading exercise only.

Consider adding a short "What entanglement gives you" callout when it first matters computationally — probably in Deep-Dive 1 when the ZZ gate creates correlations between qubits that can't be described by independent single-qubit states.

### The "exercises" gap
The SPEC mentions exercises for Phase 2. Currently, every chapter is purely expository. The notebooks partially fill this role, but a "Try this" prompt at the end of each Deep-Dive (e.g., "modify the triangle QAOA to a 4-node graph," "factor 21 instead of 15") would significantly increase engagement for the grad-student reader.

## Summary

**Strongest aspects:** The problem-first structure, the interference-as-thesis thread, the truth tables and box-level circuit diagrams, the honesty policy, and the conclusion.

**Weakest aspects:** The word-count imbalance across units, the citation-heavy Reality Checks (especially Units 1–2), the formula-density spikes in Units 5 and 7, and the underdeveloped entanglement narrative.

**Biggest single improvement:** Expanding Units 5–8 to match the narrative depth of Units 1–3. The algorithmic content is there; it just needs the same patient, concrete, show-don't-tell treatment that makes Unit 1 work so well.
