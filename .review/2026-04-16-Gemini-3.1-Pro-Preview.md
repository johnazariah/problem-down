## Specific Feedback & Areas for Improvement

### 1. Excessive Name-Dropping
*When author/academic citations and algorithm taxonomies overwhelm the narrative.*

**Unit 1 (Logistics):**
In *The Hook*, the text lists classical heuristics: *"Branch-and-bound, genetic algorithms, simulated annealing, Lin-Kernighan"*. Listing four specific algorithms in rapid succession without explaining how *any* of them intuitively work creates a momentary wall of jargon.
*Recommendation:* Keep Simulated Annealing (as it provides a great physical intuition about escaping local minima), and drop the others.

**Unit 3 (Cryptography):**
*   **The "Reality Check" is highly academic:** Executives want timelines and bottom-line resource requirements, not an academic literature review. Dropping specific authorship and preprint identifiers into the running text (e.g., "Gidney and Ekerå (2021)", "Kim, Jang, et al. (ePrint 2026/106)") creates unnecessary friction.
*   **Cryptographic Protocols:** Mentioning "CRYSTALS-Kyber (key encapsulation)" and "CRYSTALS-Dilithium (digital signatures)" by name and niche is overkill for a high-level executive summary on post-quantum migration.

**Deep-Dive 4 (Inside Shor's):**
*   **The Algorithm Bingo:** *"Deutsch-Jozsa, Bernstein-Vazirani, Simon's algorithm, Grover's search, and Shor's algorithm all use phase kickback."* Piling five algorithm names into one sentence without explaining what they actually do creates cognitive load without pedagogical payoff.
*   **Genealogy of Adders:** *"Draper's QFT-based adder, Cuccaro's ripple-carry adder, or Gidney's optimised adder."* The undergrad just needs to know that a quantum adder circuit exists and scales reasonably; giving a taxonomic list of inventors is distracting.

### 2. Concepts Introduced but Not Explained
*Instances where technical jargon is deployed without an anchoring definition or intuitive bridge.*

**Unit 1 (Logistics):**
*   **Interference:** The text boldly introduces "interference" to explain how quantum algorithms amplify the correct answers without any physical or visual intuition (e.g., "like ripples on a pond cancelling out the wrong answers").

**Unit 3 (Cryptography):**
*   **Unitary / Operator:** The text casually instructs the reader to *"apply the unitary that computes $f$."* Executives will not know what a unitary matrix is.
*   **Entanglement as a plot device:** *"The output register becomes entangled with the input register."* The concept of entanglement is dropped like a mechanical step without explaining *how* it locks the states together.
*   **Lattice Problems:** The text mentions new standards are *"based on lattice problems"* but leaves the executive completely in the dark as to what a lattice problem actually is.

**Deep-Dive 4 (Inside Shor's):**
*   **Garbage Entanglement:** Introduced and dismissed in a single bullet point: *"Leftover scratch qubits become entangled with the result — called garbage entanglement — which ruins interference."* This is a massive, fundamental hurdle in reversible computing that warrants more than a passing mention.
*   **Tensor Products:** *"The output state can be written as a tensor product of single-qubit states"* along with the $\bigotimes$ notation. This assumes the undergrad is fluent in tensor product spaces.

**Unit 17a (Error Correction):**
*   **Bloch Sphere:** *"A qubit's state is a point on the Bloch sphere..."* Instantly expects the reader to visualize the geometric representation of a qubit without prior runway.

### 3. Terminology Creep
*Shifting nomenclature that forces the reader to map multiple words to the same underlying concept.*

**Unit 1 (Logistics):**
*   **The Abrupt TSP $\rightarrow$ MaxCut Pivot:** The transition from relatable UPS truck routing to MaxCut (coloring nodes red and blue) is mathematically sound but narratively jarring for an executive. Ground MaxCut in a relatable, intuitive analogy ("social network division") before defining nodes and edges.

**Unit 3 & Deep-Dive 4 (Cryptography / Shor's):**
*   **The Period vs. Order Schism:** Introducing two distinct mathematical terms ("period" and "order") in the exact same paragraph for the exact same variable ($r$) adds needless friction. Just pick "period" and state that mathematicians call it the "order".
*   **Identity Crisis of $U_f$:** The function-evaluating block suffers from severe terminology creep. It starts as a **circuit**, morphs into a **unitary**, later becomes an **oracle**, and occasionally acts as an **operator**. Pick one user-friendly term and stick to it universally.
*   **The Naming of Helper Qubits:** The nomenclature for non-input qubits drifts wildly: **ancilla**, **output register**, **scratch work**, and **scratch qubits**.
*   **Phase/Amplitude/Angle:** The text oscillates uncomfortably between physical terms (*phase, interference*), geometric terms (*angle $\theta$, arrows*), and mathematical terms (*complex amplitudes, eigenvalues*). This forces the reader to constantly translate between trigonometry, linear algebra, and physics.

## Summary
The foundational approach is solid, but the execution needs auditing to remove stealth jargon and better bridge concepts to intuition. Ensure every introduced concept buys its way into the text by doing clear, intuitive explanatory work.
